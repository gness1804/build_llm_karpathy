ANSWER: I have so many answers fighting to get out, they're all jammed into each other. I'm just going to pull them out one at a time and probably get the order all wrong.

First, I suggest you talk to your co-workers vs. observe them. Not in a judging way, either -- do not , do not come at them with, "I can't imagine complaining that bitterly about my baby." Because defenses will go up so fast they may knock you backward. If you approach in an "obviously there's some exaggeration here but you're scaring me" way, and, "I'd love to hear some good stuff," then you have a much better chance at truth. There could be an enormous gap between what they're blurting out in moments of exasperation and what they deeply feel.

Second, as long as we're doing the "most of my friends" thing -- most of my friends (vast majority) worked while they were actively rearing their kids. And not only were most of them also happy, the relatively few unhappinesses were often not related to the combination of working outside the home and being parents. So, that it might not be "possible" is astonishing to me, really. In the way that it would be astonishing to ask, "Is is possible to be a happy breather of air?"

Yes, it's challenging. Time is an issue. But being home is challenging for different reasons. Working outside the home or not isn't what makes or breaks a household. The trick is to choose what is right for your specific circumstances -- you and your partner, if you have one, and your kids, and your finances, and so on.

So, third item. If I were to construct an is-it-possible-to-be-happy scenario, I might use this phrasing: Is it possible to be happy with an "I had always assumed ___" outlook on life? Because to me, the building blocks for a satisfying life aren't in this choice or that choice or the other demographic. They're here: in openness to making a joyful, meaningful life of what comes.

You're a few steps into that with your epiphany that you don't want to leave your job. Despite what you "had always assumed," the life course you're on has its own ideas.

So, keep going with that. Keep challenging your assumptions with reality. Your husband, for example, as a teacher, might have a schedule that aligns well enough with your child(ren)'s over the years to tame some of the frazzle. You may be able to hire help or organize better around some of the stress. You may find that "venting" is not your parent-bonding language; no judgment.
