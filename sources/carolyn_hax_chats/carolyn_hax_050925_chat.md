ANSWER: This is an unusually intimate heartbreak, I am so sorry.

Can‚Äôt make anyone feel
Guest May 9, 1:25 p.m.
QUESTION: I‚Äôve heard this- you don‚Äôt have control over anyone‚Äôs feelings. Okay, makes sense. But is the reserve true- that you are not accountable to anyone‚Äôs feelings? So if I tell someone ‚Äúyou are making me feel sad‚Äù is this wrong? Isn‚Äôt there some accountability for affecting people‚Äôs feelings? I may have been talking to someone else who misinterpreted this. I just want some clarity.
avatar
Carolyn Hax
Advice Columnist
ANSWER: Well ... as manipulators across time have always understood, there's a lot we can do to control other people's feelings.

What you're getting at is where the lines of reponsibility go. It's a really good concept to have down, because it's what differentiates healthy relationships from unhealthy ones.

Each of us is responsible for what we choose to do. That includes taking into account its possible effect on others.

But that doesn't mean anything I do that hurts someone else is wrong. I don't have to make plans with someone I don't like, for example, just because saying no would hurt them. My time is my responsibility, not theirs.

If instead I did something hurtful just to hurt, like ... I broke something of theirs out of spite, then their hard feelings would totally be my fault.

So to take your example, "You are making me feel sad." It depends on the context, but in general, it's okay to tell someone when you are feeling sad in response to their actions toward you. "When you say these things to me, I feel really sad." Even if it's not something they're doing to you, it's okay. Like if someone moves away: "I'm sad we don't see each other anymore."

But that doesn't translate into another person's responsibility to make you un-sad. You hope the person will stop saying the things to you -- but if they don't, then it's up to you to do what you need to protect yourself from that person.

I'm taking forever on this, explaining it through examples. In any situation, though, you can reason it out by tracing the baseline responsibility. Whose choice it is, whose business it is, whose time it is, who has the last word. When you're trying to get other people to make you feel better, or trying to manipulate how others feel for your own advantage, then you're crossing lines.
